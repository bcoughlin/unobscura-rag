# Unobscura Terminology Mapping
**Repository:** unobscura-rag  
**Purpose:** Maps common colloquial and clinical terms to their scientific grounding, seminal scholarship, and Unobscura's preferred framing. Intended as a knowledge base resource for RAG retrieval, content development, and consistent organizational voice.

**Framing note:** Unobscura grounds all terminology in neurodivergent-led scholarship. We reject deficit framings and "autism as competitive advantage" narratives alike. Where a term carries contested history, that context is noted.

Each entry includes:

- **Seminal works:** Key papers and books; prioritizes neurodivergent-led scholarship
- **Unobscura framing:** How we use or contextualize this term
- **Language notes:** Preferred usage, terms to avoid, contested history

### Stimming
**Also known as:** Self-stimulatory behavior, repetitive behavior, stereotypy (clinical)

**Scientific grounding:**  
Stimming refers to repetitive sensory or motor behaviors that serve regulatory functions — reducing anxiety, processing sensory input, and expressing emotion. It engages proprioceptive, vestibular, auditory, tactile, and visual systems. Neuroscientifically, stimming is understood as a self-regulation strategy: it modulates arousal levels and provides predictable sensory feedback when the external environment is unpredictable or overwhelming. All humans stim to some degree (tapping a foot, twirling hair); autistic people stim more frequently, more intensely, and with greater need for the behavior to remain uninterrupted.

Suppressing stimming has measurable psychological costs. Research links stim suppression to increased anxiety, masking burden, and autistic burnout. Stims are not symptoms to be eliminated — they are functional behaviors.

**Seminal works:**
- Kapp, S. K., Steward, R., Crane, L., et al. (2019). "People should be allowed to do what they like": Autistic adults' views and experiences of stimming. *Autism, 23*(7), 1782–1792.
- Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism.* Crown. [Chapter 1 covers stimming as regulation and the harms of suppression]
- DSM-5 (APA, 2013): Lists repetitive behaviors as diagnostic criterion B — though Unobscura notes this framing pathologizes adaptive regulation strategies.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Stimming is a regulatory behavior, not a behavioral problem. Interventions that suppress stimming (including many ABA-derived approaches) prioritize neurotypical comfort over autistic wellbeing. Unobscura uses the term without clinical qualifiers and does not treat stimming as something requiring management.

**Language notes:**
- Prefer: *stimming*, *self-regulation behavior*
- Avoid: *stereotypy* (clinical; dehumanizing in lay contexts), *self-injurious behavior* (only accurate for a small subset and conflates the category)
- Note: "Stimming" is community-originated language; use it.

### Masking / Camouflaging
**Also known as:** Camouflaging, passing, social performance, code-switching (partial overlap)

**Scientific grounding:**  
Masking refers to the effortful suppression or modification of autistic traits in order to appear neurotypical. It is a coping strategy, not a natural social skill. Research distinguishes three components: *assimilation* (hiding autistic traits), *compensation* (substituting scripted behaviors), and *masking* (overall performance of neurotypicality). Masking is cognitively expensive — it requires simultaneous monitoring of one's own behavior, the environment, and other people's responses, in real time.

Masking is associated with significant mental health costs including depression, anxiety, autistic burnout, suicidality, and delayed diagnosis. It is more prevalent in women, girls, and non-binary people, contributing to diagnostic disparities.

**Seminal works:**
- Hull, L., Petrides, K. V., Allison, C., et al. (2017). "Putting on My Best Normal": Social Camouflaging in Adults with Autism Spectrum Conditions. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 47*, 2519–2534.
- Cage, E., & Troxell-Whitman, Z. (2019). Understanding the reasons, contexts and costs of camouflaging for autistic adults. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49*, 1899–1911.
- Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism.* Crown. [Central theme throughout]

**Unobscura framing:**  
Masking is a survival strategy developed in response to environments that do not accept autistic communication and behavior. It is not a choice or a skill — it is a tax imposed on neurodivergent people by neurotypical social norms. Unobscura connects masking directly to hiring contexts: candidate screening formats that require real-time social performance impose a masking burden that obscures genuine competence.

**Language notes:**
- *Masking* and *camouflaging* are both acceptable; *masking* is more widely used in community contexts
- Avoid: *"faking it"*, *"pretending to be normal"* — these imply deception rather than adaptive survival
- *Code-switching* has specific meaning in linguistics and racial identity contexts; don't conflate

### Autistic Burnout
**Also known as:** Burnout, regression (clinical; outdated and rejected), "hitting a wall"

**Scientific grounding:**  
Autistic burnout is a state of chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced tolerance to stimuli resulting from sustained masking, sensory overload, and the accumulated effort of navigating neurotypical environments. It is distinct from occupational burnout. Raymaker et al. (2020) identified three core features: *pervasive exhaustion*, *loss of skills*, and *reduced tolerance to stimuli*, often following prolonged periods of high social demand.

Burnout can last months or years and may be mistaken for depression, regression, or new-onset psychiatric illness. Late-diagnosed adults are at particular risk due to decades of unrecognized masking.

**Seminal works:**
- Raymaker, D. M., Teo, A. R., Steckler, N. A., et al. (2020). "Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew": Defining Autistic Burnout. *Autism in Adulthood, 2*(2), 132–143.
- Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism.* Crown.
- Higgins, J. M., Arnold, S., Weise, J., et al. (2021). Defining autistic burnout through experts by lived experience. *Autism, 25*(5), 1294–1306.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Burnout is a systems-level outcome, not an individual failure. It is caused by environments that demand continuous masking and offer insufficient accommodation. Recognizing burnout as structurally produced is essential to Unobscura's prevention-through-understanding approach.

**Language notes:**
- Avoid: *regression* — implies developmental reversal; is clinically inaccurate and carries stigma
- "Hitting a wall" is community-used and acceptable in informal contexts

### Double Empathy Problem
**Also known as:** DEP, bidirectional communication differences, cross-neurotype communication

**Scientific grounding:**  
The Double Empathy Problem (DEP), theorized by Damian Milton (2012), proposes that social and communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are *bidirectional* — neither party fully understands the other. This directly challenges the deficit model, which locates the problem entirely in the autistic person. Empirical support has grown substantially: autistic people communicate effectively with each other; it is specifically cross-neurotype communication that breaks down. This has direct implications for how "social skills deficits" are interpreted and assessed.

**Seminal works:**
- Milton, D. E. M. (2012). On the ontological status of autism: The 'double empathy problem.' *Disability & Society, 27*(6), 883–887.
- Crompton, C. J., Ropar, D., Evans-Williams, C. V. M., et al. (2020). Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective. *Autism, 24*(7), 1704–1712.
- Morrison, K. E., DeBrabander, K. M., Jones, D. R., et al. (2020). Outcomes of real-world social interaction for autistic adults paired with autistic or non-autistic partners. *Autism, 24*(5), 1356–1370.

**Unobscura framing:**  
The DEP is the theoretical cornerstone of Unobscura's work. It reframes hiring failures, workplace misunderstandings, and family conflict as cross-neurotype communication breakdowns rather than neurodivergent deficits. Assessment systems built around allistic communication norms will systematically undercount autistic competence — not because autistic candidates lack skill, but because the measurement instrument is calibrated to the wrong standard.

**Language notes:**
- Use *Double Empathy Problem* on first reference; *DEP* is acceptable in subsequent references in technical content
- Avoid: any framing that positions autistic people as the sole source of communication breakdown

### Monotropism
**Also known as:** Attention tunnel, interest-based attention, hyperfocus (partial overlap)

**Scientific grounding:**  
Monotropism is a theory of autism developed by Dinah Murray, Wenn Lawson, and later expanded by Damian Milton. It proposes that autistic cognition involves a strong tendency to focus attention on fewer things at once, but with greater depth and intensity. The "interest system" drives attention, and disrupting a monotropic flow state (e.g., with transitions, interruptions, or context-switching demands) produces the distress and resistance often labeled as "rigidity" or "behavioral problems."

Monotropism offers an explanatory model for many autistic experiences: special interests, difficulty with transitions, executive function differences, sensory sensitivity, and social communication differences can all be understood through the lens of attention distribution.

**Seminal works:**
- Murray, D., Lesser, M., & Lawson, W. (2005). Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism. *Autism, 9*(2), 136–156.
- Lawson, W. (2011). *The Passionate Mind: How People with Autism Learn.* Jessica Kingsley.
- Milton, D., & Murray, D. (2016). Monotropism. In *The SAGE Handbook of Autism and Education.*

**Unobscura framing:**  
Monotropism explains why timed, context-switching-heavy assessments systematically disadvantage autistic candidates — the format itself conflicts with how monotropic attention works. It also explains the depth of expertise autistic people often develop in their domains of focus. This is not a deficit; it is a different cognitive architecture that current assessment systems fail to accommodate.

**Language notes:**
- *Hyperfocus* is widely used in ADHD communities and has partial overlap with monotropic flow states, but the mechanisms are distinct; don't treat them as synonymous
- *Special interests* is community-accepted; *obsessions* (clinical) carries pathologizing connotation

### Interoception
**Also known as:** Internal body sense, body awareness, the "eighth sense"

**Scientific grounding:**  
Interoception is the sensory system that perceives internal body states — hunger, thirst, heart rate, temperature, pain, fatigue, and emotional arousal. Research shows autistic people frequently have atypical interoceptive processing: signals may be absent, delayed, muted, or arrive as undifferentiated distress rather than clearly labeled sensations. This has downstream effects on emotional awareness, self-regulation, and the ability to identify and communicate internal states.

Interoceptive differences help explain why autistic people may not notice hunger until severely hungry, may not recognize anxiety until it reaches crisis level, or may have difficulty identifying what emotion they are experiencing (see also: *alexithymia*).

**Seminal works:**
- Schauder, K. B., Mash, L. E., Bryant, L. K., & Cascio, C. J. (2015). Interoceptive ability and body awareness in autism spectrum disorder. *Journal of Experimental Child Psychology, 131*, 193–200.
- Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism.* Crown. [Chapter on interoception and emotional self-knowledge]
- Mahler, K. (2017). *Interoception: The Eighth Sensory System.* AAPC Publishing.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Interoceptive differences are frequently invisible in professional contexts — they don't present as a visible disability but significantly affect capacity for self-regulation, stress management, and sustained performance. Understanding interoception helps employers move beyond surface-level accommodation ("do you need a quiet room?") toward more meaningful support.

### Alexithymia
**Also known as:** Emotional blindness, difficulty identifying feelings, emotional processing differences

**Scientific grounding:**  
Alexithymia is a trait characterized by difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotional states. It is present in approximately 50% of autistic people (compared to ~10% in the general population), but is not unique to autism — it also occurs in non-autistic people and in trauma responses. Alexithymia and autism are distinct but frequently co-occurring. Historically, alexithymia-related presentations were misattributed to "lack of empathy" in autistic people; research has substantially revised this interpretation.

**Seminal works:**
- Poquérusse, J., Pastore, L., Dellantonio, S., & Esposito, G. (2018). Alexithymia and Autism Spectrum Disorder: A Complex Relationship. *Frontiers in Psychology, 9*, 1196.
- Cook, R., Brewer, R., Shah, P., & Bird, G. (2013). Alexithymia, not autism, predicts poor recognition of emotional facial expressions. *Psychological Science, 24*(5), 723–732.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Alexithymia is frequently conflated with "lack of empathy" in autistic people — a characterization Unobscura explicitly rejects. Difficulty labeling one's own emotions is not the same as not having them or not caring about others. This conflation has caused significant harm in clinical, professional, and personal relationship contexts.

**Language notes:**
- Avoid: *"lack of empathy"* as a description of autistic people — this is empirically contested and harmful
- Alexithymia is a trait, not a disorder; use trait language

### Echolalia
**Also known as:** Scripting, echoing, scripted speech, delayed echolalia

**Scientific grounding:**  
Echolalia is the repetition of words, phrases, or passages heard previously — immediately (*immediate echolalia*) or after a delay (*delayed echolalia*). It is a form of functional communication, not meaningless repetition. Research by Barry Prizant and others reconceptualized echolalia from a "symptom" to a meaningful communicative strategy: it can convey needs, regulate emotional states, express affiliation, or serve as a bridge when novel language construction is cognitively unavailable under stress.

**Seminal works:**
- Prizant, B. M., & Duchan, J. F. (1981). The functions of immediate echolalia in autistic children. *Journal of Speech and Hearing Disorders, 46*(3), 241–249.
- Prizant, B. M. (2015). *Uniquely Human: A Different Way of Seeing Autism.* Simon & Schuster.
- Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism.* Crown.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Echolalia is communication — it should be understood as such, not pathologized or suppressed. In professional contexts, scripted communication (preparing specific phrases for specific situations) is a legitimate and effective strategy, not a workaround to be ashamed of.

### Neurodiversity / Neurodivergent
**Also known as:** Neurological diversity, cognitive diversity (Unobscura preferred in some contexts)

**Scientific grounding:**  
*Neurodiversity* is a concept coined by sociologist Judy Singer in 1998, referring to the natural variation in human neurological function. It frames neurological differences (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, etc.) as part of human variation rather than pathology. *Neurodivergent* describes an individual whose neurological function diverges from dominant norms. The Neurodiversity Paradigm, as articulated by Nick Walker, holds that neurological diversity is a natural and valuable feature of the human species, and that what is disabling is primarily the mismatch between neurodivergent people and environments built for a narrow neurotype.

**Seminal works:**
- Singer, J. (1998). *Odd People In: The Birth of Community Amongst People on the Autistic Spectrum.* [Thesis; coinage of "neurodiversity"]
- Walker, N. (2021). *Neuroqueer Heresies: Notes on the Neurodiversity Paradigm, Autistic Empowerment, and Postnormal Possibilities.* Autonomous Press.
- Armstrong, T. (2010). *Neurodiversity: Discovering the Extraordinary Gifts of Autism, ADHD, Dyslexia, and Other Brain Differences.* Da Capo Press.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Unobscura uses *neurodivergent* and *neurotype* as standard vocabulary. We prefer *cognitive diversity* in some public-facing contexts to make the concept accessible without requiring familiarity with clinical or community terminology. We do not use neurodiversity as a synonym for autism specifically — it is a population-level descriptor.

**Language notes:**
- *Neurodiverse* is sometimes misused to describe an individual; technically, a population is neurodiverse, an individual is neurodivergent
- Avoid: *special needs*, *differently abled* — these are rejected by most of the community (see Price, 2022)
- Identity-first language (*autistic person*) is preferred by most autistic adults over person-first (*person with autism*)

### Autistic Inertia
**Also known as:** Task initiation difficulty, starting problem, transitions difficulty, "stuck" states

**Scientific grounding:**  
Autistic inertia refers to difficulty initiating, stopping, or switching between tasks or states — not due to laziness or willfulness, but due to neurological differences in how action and attention are initiated and maintained. It is related to monotropism (deep attentional focus that resists interruption) and to executive function differences. Inertia affects both starting tasks (initiation inertia) and stopping them (perseveration). It is frequently misread as noncompliance, avoidance, or low motivation.

**Seminal works:**
- Leary, M. R., & Hill, D. A. (1996). Moving on: Autism and movement disturbance. *Mental Retardation, 34*(1), 39–53.
- Donnellan, A. M., Hill, D. A., & Leary, M. R. (2013). Rethinking autism: Implications of sensory and movement differences. *Frontiers in Integrative Neuroscience, 6*, 124.
- Buckle, K. L., Leadbitter, K., Poliakoff, E., & Gowen, E. (2021). "No way out except from external intervention": First-hand accounts of autistic inertia. *Frontiers in Psychology, 12*, 631596.
- Arnold, L. (2019). Autistic Inertia. *Autistic UK.* [Community resource; accessible overview]

**Unobscura framing:**  
Autistic inertia is central to the Unstuck Framework. It is a neurological reality, not a character flaw. Environments that demand rapid task initiation, frequent context-switching, or real-time pivots systematically disadvantage autistic people with inertia — without this being visible in any diagnostic assessment. Unobscura treats inertia as a workplace and assessment design issue, not a candidate deficiency.

### Support Needs
**Also known as:** Level of support, functioning level (outdated), high/low functioning (rejected)

**Scientific grounding:**  
*Support needs* describes the degree of support a person requires in various domains of life, recognizing that needs vary by context, task, and environment. It replaces the "functioning" language (high-functioning, low-functioning) that dominated autism discourse through the 1990s–2010s. Functioning labels are empirically problematic: they are inconsistent across contexts, conflate support needs with intellectual ability, and are used to both dismiss support needs ("you're too high-functioning to need help") and underestimate capability ("they're too low-functioning to have preferences").

**Seminal works:**
- Doyle, N. (2020). Neurodiversity at work: A biopsychosocial model and the impact on working adults. *British Medical Bulletin, 135*(1), 108–125.
- Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism.* Crown. [Chapter 1: terminology table]
- Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN): Position statements on functioning labels.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Unobscura uses *support needs* and, where specificity is needed, describes what those needs are concretely rather than assigning a global label. We reject functioning labels entirely. A person is not high- or low-functioning; they have varying support needs across different contexts.

**Language notes:**
- Prefer: *higher support needs / lower support needs* (if level must be indicated)
- Avoid: *high-functioning*, *low-functioning*, *mild autism*, *severe autism* — all are contested and often harmful
- *Support person* is preferred over *caregiver* in contexts where the person receiving support is an adult with agency

### Executive Function
**Also known as:** EF, executive dysfunction, cognitive control, self-regulation (partial overlap)

**Scientific grounding:**  
Executive function refers to a cluster of higher-order cognitive processes managed largely by the prefrontal cortex: working memory, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control, planning, task initiation, and emotional regulation. Autistic people and ADHDers commonly experience executive function differences — not uniform deficits, but uneven profiles. A person may have exceptional capacity in some EF domains (sustained attention on high-interest tasks) and significant difficulty in others (task initiation, time perception, working memory under social load).

**Seminal works:**
- Hill, E. L. (2004). Evaluating the theory of executive dysfunction in autism. *Developmental Review, 24*(2), 189–233.
- Barkley, R. A. (2012). *Executive Functions: What They Are, How They Work, and Why They Evolved.* Guilford Press.
- Ozonoff, S., & Jensen, J. (1999). Specific executive function profiles in three neurodevelopmental disorders. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 29*(2), 171–177.

**Unobscura framing:**  
Executive function differences are invisible in standard assessments unless the assessment is specifically designed to surface them — and even then, results are context-dependent. A candidate who struggles with cold-start task initiation in a timed assessment may perform with high fluency in their actual work environment. Unobscura uses EF as an explanatory lens, not a deficit label.

### Neurodiversity Paradigm
**Also known as:** Social model of neurodevelopment, neurodivergent-affirmative framework

**Scientific grounding:**  
The Neurodiversity Paradigm, articulated most systematically by Nick Walker, holds that neurological diversity is a natural form of human variation; that there is no single "correct" way for a human brain to be structured or to function; and that the concept of "neurological normality" is a social construct that reflects the dominance of one neurotype rather than an objective standard. It complements the social model of disability — disability is created by the interaction between an individual's characteristics and an environment built without them in mind.

**Seminal works:**
- Walker, N. (2021). *Neuroqueer Heresies.* Autonomous Press.
- Walker, N. (2014). Neurodiversity: Some Basic Terms and Definitions. *Neurocosmopolitanism.* [Blog; widely cited foundational text]
- Woods, R., Milton, D., Arnold, L., & Graby, S. (2018). Redefining critical autism studies: A more inclusive interpretation. *Disability & Society, 33*(6), 974–979.

**Unobscura framing:**  
The Neurodiversity Paradigm is the philosophical foundation beneath Unobscura's applied work. It is the *why* behind every tool, framework, and advocacy position we develop.

### Sensory Processing Differences
**Also known as:** Sensory sensitivity, sensory processing disorder (SPD), sensory integration dysfunction, hyper/hyposensitivity

**Scientific grounding:**  
Sensory processing differences in autism involve atypical responses to sensory input across multiple modalities: auditory, visual, tactile, olfactory, gustatory, proprioceptive, vestibular, and interoceptive. Responses may be hypersensitive (overreactive), hyposensitive (underreactive), or variable. The DSM-5 includes sensory differences in its diagnostic criteria for autism (Criterion B4). Sensory differences are not psychological — they reflect differences in how sensory signals are filtered, integrated, and interpreted in the nervous system.

**Seminal works:**
- Marco, E. J., Hinkley, L. B. N., Hill, S. S., & Nagarajan, S. S. (2011). Sensory processing in autism: A review of neurophysiologic findings. *Pediatric Research, 69*, 48–54.
- Ayres, A. J. (1972). *Sensory Integration and Learning Disorders.* Western Psychological Services. [Historical; foundational]
- Grandin, T. (1995). *Thinking in Pictures.* Doubleday. [Lived experience account; widely cited]

**Unobscura framing:**  
Sensory differences affect workplace functioning in ways that are often invisible and unaddressed because they are not accommodated unless disclosed — and disclosure requires trusting that the environment is safe. Unobscura frames sensory accommodation as a basic design question (like ergonomics), not an exceptional request.

## Terminology To Avoid

| Avoid | Prefer | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| High-functioning / Low-functioning | Higher/lower support needs | Conflates IQ with support needs; used to dismiss or underestimate |
| Person with autism | Autistic person | Most autistic adults prefer identity-first |
| Special needs | Support needs | Condescending; avoids specificity |
| Differently abled | Disabled / neurodivergent | Euphemistic; rejected by disability community |
| Suffers from autism | Is autistic | Autism is not inherently suffering |
| Mild / severe autism | Varying support needs | Minimizes or amplifies in misleading ways |
| Disorder | Difference, divergence (context-dependent) | "Disorder" locates the problem in the person |
| Regression | Burnout, skill loss during burnout | Regression implies developmental reversal |
| Lacks empathy | Has alexithymia / cross-neurotype communication differences | Empirically inaccurate; harmful |
| Obsession | Special interest, area of deep focus | Pathologizes a neutral or positive trait |
| Caregiver (for adults) | Support person | Adults have agency; caregiver implies dependency |
