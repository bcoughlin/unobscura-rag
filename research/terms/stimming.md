# Stimming: a comprehensive guide to self-stimulatory behavior

**Stimming — the repetitive performance of movements, sounds, or other sensory-rich behaviors — is a fundamental aspect of autistic neurology, not a symptom to be eliminated.** Rooted in sensory modulation, emotional regulation, and cognitive processing, stimming serves as a homeostatic mechanism that helps autistic people navigate a world built for different nervous systems. The term itself was reclaimed by autistic communities in the 1990s from the clinical language of Applied Behavior Analysis, transforming "self-stimulatory behavior" from a target for elimination into a celebrated expression of neurodivergent identity. Decades of research — increasingly led by autistic scholars — now confirm what the community has long known: suppressing stimming causes measurable psychological harm, while accepting it supports wellbeing, self-regulation, and authentic connection.

This document synthesizes neuroscience, autistic-led scholarship, community history, and clinical critique to provide a neurodivergent-affirming resource on stimming. It prioritizes autistic voices, empirical evidence, and the principle that understanding stimming begins with listening to those who do it.

## Where "stimming" came from and how it was reclaimed

The word **"stimming"** is a clipped form of "self-stimming," itself shortened from "self-stimulatory behavior" (SSB). The Oxford English Dictionary records the **earliest known use of the noun "stimming" in 1983**, in the *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis*, where it described "nonvocal, inappropriate repetitive movement consisting of picking, tapping or spinning objects, hand flapping, stroking lips, hand clenching accompanied by facial contortions, or running back and forth." Dictionary.com dates first recorded use to 1980–85.

The clinical term "self-stimulatory behavior" emerged from the **Applied Behavior Analysis tradition at UCLA** in the late 1960s and early 1970s, primarily through the work of O. Ivar Lovaas and colleagues. Key early publications include Lovaas, Litrownik, and Mann (1971) on "response latencies to auditory stimuli in autistic children engaged in self-stimulatory behavior" in *Behaviour Research and Therapy*, and Koegel and Covert (1972) in *JABA*, which used punishment — including "slapping the subject briskly on the hands" — to suppress what they termed "self-stimulation." Tony Attwood has noted that the term was coined to describe behaviors observed in autistic people "living on a ward in a government institution with spartan furniture, almost no recreational facilities and staff who were not trained." The clinical framework attributed these behaviors to individual pathology rather than environmental deprivation.

**Autistic self-advocates reclaimed "stimming" in online community spaces during the 1990s and early 2000s**, through forums like Autism Network International (ANI), founded by Jim Sinclair, and later through the Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN). As Kapp et al. (2019) documented, "Autistic adults have led resistance to these efforts [to eliminate stims], reclaiming 'self-stimulatory behaviour' as 'stimming.'" This reclamation reframed stimming from a dysfunctional symptom into a purposeful, adaptive behavior serving legitimate neurological needs — self-regulation, emotional expression, sensory processing, and communication. Scholar-activists including Mel Yergeau (2016) challenged declarations of stimming as necessarily involuntary, while Nolan and McBride (2015) denounced attempts to reduce autistic bodily autonomy.

The related clinical term **"stereotypy"** (from Greek *stereos* + *typos*, "fixed impression") has been used in psychiatry since the 19th century to describe repetitive, seemingly purposeless movements. It is considered problematic by many autistic people because it originates in B.F. Skinner's behavioral tradition, implies purposelessness (directly contradicting autistic testimony), and is deeply intertwined with ABA practices aimed at elimination. The term "restricted and repetitive behaviors" (RRBs) became the standard clinical umbrella from DSM-IV onward, though the neurodiversity community critiques the word "restricted" as deficit-framing of deep, focused interests.

## What happens in the brain during stimming

The neuroscience of stimming involves multiple interconnected brain systems, neurotransmitter pathways, and sensory processing mechanisms. While research is ongoing, a coherent picture has emerged of stimming as a neurologically grounded regulatory behavior.

### The neural circuitry

The **basal ganglia** are central to stimming. Cortico-striatal projections to the dorsal striatum convey input from motor, cognitive, and limbic cortices. Neuroimaging studies have identified increased striatal volume — particularly the **caudate nucleus** — correlating with repetitive behaviors in autism (Hollander et al., 2005; Langen et al., 2009, 2014). Grossberg and Kishnan (2018, *Frontiers in Psychology*) proposed that when basal ganglia gates in the substantia nigra remain open too long, downstream recurrent circuits persistently oscillate, triggering repetitive motor behaviors. Postmortem studies found **increased D2 receptor expression** in medium spiny neurons of the caudate and putamen in autistic individuals (Brandenburg et al., 2020, *Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience*), implicating the indirect basal ganglia pathway.

The **cerebellum** plays a significant role. Singer et al. (2021, *The Cerebellum*) found statistically significant reduction in white matter volume of posterior cerebellar lobules VI–VII that negatively correlated with motor control, alongside an 8% increase in anterior vermis gray matter positively correlated with stereotypy severity scores. The **prefrontal cortex** is implicated through executive processing and dopamine-based modulation, with fronto-striatal functional connectivity showing significant positive association with repetitive behavior severity. Research from the University of Rochester (Isenstein et al., *NeuroImage*) using EEG found that autistic participants showed reduced predictive attenuation during self-initiated touch compared to neurotypical controls — potentially driving the need for repetitive, predictable self-generated sensory feedback.

### Neurotransmitter systems

**Dopamine** is the most extensively studied neurotransmitter in relation to stimming. The dopamine hypothesis of autism (Pavăl, 2017, *Developmental Neuroscience*) posits that nigrostriatal circuit dysfunction is associated with stereotyped behaviors, while mesocorticolimbic dysfunction relates to social differences. A Karolinska Institutet study (Santini et al.) demonstrated that mice with the autism-risk gene eIF4E showed reduced dopamine release in the basal ganglia due to impaired acetylcholine-dopamine interactions. Stimming likely stimulates dopamine release, which can decrease glutamate levels, calming the brain and providing comfort. Because stimming produces both sensory feedback and neurochemical reward, it becomes self-reinforcing.

**GABA**, the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter, shows **reduced levels** in autism — a central feature of the excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) imbalance theory. Lower sensorimotor GABA levels are associated with tactile hypersensitivity (Sapey-Triomphe et al., 2019, *Frontiers in Neuroscience*). Reduced GABA in the left ventral premotor cortex correlates with sensory hyper-responsiveness (Puts et al., 2017, *Autism Research*). This E/I imbalance helps explain why autistic nervous systems are more reactive to sensory input and correspondingly more dependent on regulatory strategies like stimming.

The **endogenous opioid system** offers another mechanism: the endorphin hypothesis proposes that certain stimming actions trigger beta-endorphin release, producing pleasurable feelings and pain reduction. Studies of opioid antagonists (naloxone, naltrexone) in autistic individuals showed reduction in self-aggressive behavior and hyperactivity, supporting this pathway (reviewed in Ferrara, 2020, *Brain Sciences*). **Serotonin** also plays a role, with hyperserotonemia found in approximately 25–30% of autistic individuals (Gabriele et al., 2014, *European Neuropsychopharmacology*).

### Stimming as arousal regulation

Stimming functions as arousal regulation consistent with the **Yerkes-Dodson inverted-U model**: when overstimulated, stimming blocks or modulates sensory input to bring arousal down; when understimulated, it provides additional input to raise arousal toward the optimal zone. Ornitz and Ritvo (1968) described autism as a syndrome of "perceptual inconstancy," with motor output needed to modulate inconsistent sensory input. More recent predictive processing frameworks (Pellicano and Burr, 2012, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*; Lawson, Rees, and Friston, 2014, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*) suggest stimming provides familiar, reliable self-generated feedback in response to difficulties with unpredictable stimuli.

**Sensory hyperresponsiveness predicts approximately 51% of the variance in repetitive behaviors**, with sensory-seeking behaviors accounting for an additional 11% (Zetler et al., 2022). Kapp et al. (2019) found that **72% of autistic adults** report stimming helps with anxiety, **57%** with overstimulation, and **69%** to calm down.

### Sensory processing across the spectrum

Winnie Dunn's (1997) Four Quadrant Model provides a foundational framework for understanding stimming across sensory profiles, organized along two axes — neurological threshold (high to low) and behavioral response (passive to active):

- **Sensation seeking** (high threshold, active response): Actively pursuing more sensory input — the "I need more" state that drives stimming for arousal
- **Sensation avoiding** (low threshold, active response): Actively limiting sensory input — the "too much" state where stimming blocks overwhelming stimuli
- **Sensory sensitivity** (low threshold, passive response): Detecting more input and becoming easily distracted
- **Low registration** (high threshold, passive response): Not noticing stimuli, requiring stronger input

Studies using the Sensory Profile consistently find significantly higher sensory processing differences in autistic individuals, with hearing and touch showing the most marked patterns (Tomchek and Dunn, 2007, *American Journal of Occupational Therapy*). The same individual may stim differently depending on their current sensory state — seeking proprioceptive input through jumping when under-aroused, or rocking rhythmically to create predictable input when overwhelmed.

### The autonomic nervous system and polyvagal connections

Stephen Porges' **Polyvagal Theory** proposes three hierarchical neural circuits: the ventral vagal complex (social engagement, safety), sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight), and dorsal vagal complex (shutdown, immobilization). Applied to stimming, the theory suggests that autistic people may use self-stimulatory behaviors to calm their sympathetic nervous system and maintain regulation (Hirstein, Iversen, and Ramachandran, 2001). Vestibular stimulation and proprioceptive input may serve as "neural exercises of state regulation."

However, important caveats exist. A 2022 systematic review (Benevides et al., *Frontiers in Psychiatry*) argued that autonomic nervous system dysfunction may not be a dominant feature of autism, finding most autistic children have normal parasympathetic baseline values. From the neurodiversity perspective, autistic scholar-activist "Trauma Geek" offers a critical reframe: while the core concepts of polyvagal theory remain useful, the idea that autism equals a deficit of social engagement function pathologizes autistic people. They argue autistic people may have "highly attuned or sensitive neuroception" rather than "faulty neuroception," and that low vagal function often reflects trauma history rather than inherent trait.

### How autistic stimming differs from neurotypical fidgeting

**All humans stim.** Foot tapping, hair twirling, pen clicking, and nail biting are universal self-regulatory behaviors. The difference between autistic stimming and neurotypical fidgeting is best understood as one of **degree, intensity, breadth, and functional necessity** rather than of kind:

- **Intensity and frequency**: Neurotypical fidgeting is usually short-lived and less noticeable; autistic stimming is often more sustained and visible
- **Breadth of sensory involvement**: Fidgeting primarily involves tactile stimulation, while autistic stimming encompasses auditory, visual, vestibular, proprioceptive, and olfactory senses
- **Physiological necessity**: Autistic people describe stimming as a physiological need; suppression requires extraordinary effort and causes distress. Lyric Rivera (Neurodivergent Rebel) distinguishes involuntary stimming — "part of Autistic body language" — from intentional sensory seeking, noting that suppressing involuntary stims causes physical harm (jaw pain, muscle tension, headaches)
- **Underlying neurology**: Autistic brains have fundamentally different sensory processing thresholds — lower GABA, altered dopamine signaling, different connectivity patterns — creating both greater need for self-regulation and more intense expression of regulatory behaviors

The underlying mechanisms (dopamine regulation, arousal optimization) operate in all humans, but the sensory environment is experienced more intensely by autistic people, making self-regulatory mechanisms correspondingly more essential.

## The sensory landscape of stimming types

Stimming spans every sensory modality. Understanding the full range — rather than fixating on the most visible forms — is essential for recognizing and supporting autistic self-regulation.

**Visual stimming** engages the sense of sight: moving fingers in front of eyes to filter light, watching spinning objects (fans, wheels, coins), staring at patterns or flowing water, excessive blinking, turning lights on and off, gazing at lava lamps, and lining up objects for visual satisfaction.

**Auditory stimming** engages hearing: humming tunes, listening to the same song on repeat, clicking or snapping fingers, tapping ears or objects, covering and uncovering ears, and making repetitive sounds. Auditory processing in autism shows atypical patterns, with the nervous system processing environmental sounds differently (Gonçalves and Monteiro, 2023, *Journal of Neural Transmission*).

**Tactile stimming** engages touch: rubbing textures, stroking particular fabrics, skin picking, playing with fidget toys, running fingers over surfaces, squeezing objects, and hair twirling. Hearing and touch show the most pronounced sensory processing differences in autism (Ashburner et al., 2008; Kientz and Dunn, 1997; Rogers et al., 2003).

**Vestibular stimming** engages balance and spatial orientation: body rocking (back and forth, side to side), spinning the whole body, swinging, bouncing on toes or trampolines, pacing, and head rolling. The rhythm of rocking behaviors is often around **60 beats per minute** — possibly synchronizing with and reducing heart rate (Attwood and Garnett).

**Proprioceptive stimming** engages body awareness through muscles and joints: jumping, seeking deep pressure (bear hugs, weighted blankets), clenching and relaxing muscles, pressing against walls, heavy lifting, wearing tight clothing or weighted vests, toe walking, and crashing into soft surfaces.

**Olfactory and gustatory stimming** engages smell and taste: sniffing objects, licking surfaces, chewing (chewable jewelry, pencils, gum), biting nails or lips, tasting textures, and grinding teeth. Oral stimming is particularly common and is served by a growing market of chewable stim tools.

**Vocal and echolalic stimming** engages voice and speech patterns: echolalia (repeating heard speech, present in **75–80% of verbal autistic individuals** per Prizant, 1983), scripting (repeating lines from media), palilalia (repeating one's own words), humming melodies, and making repetitive vocalizations. Modern research has overturned the view that echolalia is "meaningless repetition." Pascual et al. (2023, *PMC*) found that **all echolalia usages by autistic participants were functional and communicative**. Prizant and Duchan (1981, *Journal of Speech and Hearing Disorders*) identified functions including turn-taking, affirmation, requests, and self-regulation.

**Interoceptive dimensions** involve awareness of internal body states — heartbeat, hunger, pain, temperature. Impaired interoceptive processing in autism may contribute to difficulty identifying emotional states (alexithymia), and stimming may serve as a way to generate detectable sensory feedback when internal signals are unclear.

## What stimming actually does: regulation, expression, cognition, and joy

Stimming is not one thing. It is a multi-functional behavior that serves at least six distinct purposes, often simultaneously.

### Self-regulation and arousal modulation

This is the most extensively documented function. Kapp et al. (2019) found "a robust pattern emerged of stimming as a self-regulatory mechanism, which acted to create a calming feedback loop." Temple Grandin described how spinning a coin created such intense focus that "no sound intruded on my fixation," reducing auditory and visual sensitivity (Grandin and Scariano, 1986). Neurologically, stimming may entrain brain rhythms to enhance information processing (McCarty and Brumback, 2021), and the beta-endorphin hypothesis suggests it triggers opioid release, producing calming or euphoric effects. From an occupational therapy perspective, input to the proprioceptive, tactile, and vestibular systems plays a crucial role in nervous system regulation (Dunn, 1997; Lane et al., 2019).

### Emotional expression across the full spectrum

Stimming expresses the full range of emotions — not just distress. Morris et al. (2025, *Autism and Developmental Language Impairments*) found participants were most likely to stim when feeling **anxious (50%), frustrated (39%), excited (37%), and angry (33%)**. When excited, 38.7% reported hand flapping. Kapp et al. (2019) documented that "intense emotions could have either a positive or a negative valence, and the corresponding stims could have a different manifestation depending on valence." A "happy hand flap" — hands open, arms out in a waving-like motion — looks and feels different from a distress flap, with hands and arms kept closer to the body. Julia Bascom captured this: "I pity anyone who cannot feel the way that flapping your hands just so amplifies everything you feel and thrusts it up into the air."

### Communication and social connection

An emerging body of research reveals stimming's communicative function. Morris et al. (2025) found that **73% of autistic participants** agreed they could usually tell what other autistic people were feeling from their stims. **56%** said stimming was important to their friendships with other autistic people, and **63%** said it helped them connect with other neurodivergent people. NeuroClastic, an autistic-led resource, notes: "If a person is stimming while interacting with you, it is likely they feel safe and comfortable enough to stim — view it as a compliment." This finding connects to Damian Milton's (2012) double empathy problem: non-autistic people's failure to read stimming as meaningful communication reflects their empathy gap, not just autistic people's social "deficits."

### Stimming as thinking — flow states and focus

Abrahamson et al. (2024, *Educational Psychology Review*) published a landmark paper arguing that **"stimming is an intrinsic part of adaptive functioning, interaction, and cognitive dynamics"** — reframing it as an epistemic (knowledge-building) resource rather than a distraction from learning. They proposed three educational heuristics: offering stim affordances ("instrumenting stimming"), legitimizing stims as discursive resources ("interactional stimming"), and leveraging stims as epistemic actions ("instrumentalizing stimming"). Heasman et al. (2024, *Journal for the Theory of Social Behaviour*) developed "autistic flow theory," exploring how monotropic attention — deep, sustained focus — connects to flow states, with stimming filtering out input that threatens to break concentration.

### Pleasure as a complete explanation

Sometimes stimming requires no functional explanation at all. In a study cited by the Cleveland Clinic, **80% of participants said they stim simply because they like the way it makes them feel.** Sean Barron described: "I loved repetition. Every time I turned on a light, I knew what would happen... It gave me a wonderful sense of security because it was exactly the same each time." The neurochemistry supports this: dopamine and endorphin release during stimming creates intrinsic sensory pleasure. Stimming for enjoyment is not less valid than stimming for regulation — it is a complete reason in itself.

## The documented harm of stim suppression

The evidence on stim suppression is unambiguous: it causes psychological damage, contributes to burnout, and is associated with serious mental health consequences including suicidality. This section details the research.

### The cognitive and emotional cost

**Charlton et al. (2021)** surveyed 340 adults and found that **84% of autistic adults had been told not to stim at some point in their lives**. 75% said they did not always stim in their preferred way because they perceived it was not socially acceptable. Suppressing stims was described as **"holding back something you need to say."** Substitute stims selected to be less obvious were more effortful to maintain and less effective for self-regulation. Charlton et al. (2023) further found that when autistic people were unable to stim, they reported **significantly lower self-efficacy** than non-autistic people — a gap that disappeared when stimming was permitted.

Kapp et al. (2019) invoked Baumeister's (2002) ego depletion model, describing self-control as a limited resource. When autistic people spend cognitive energy suppressing stims, their capacity for other tasks — attention, executive function, emotional regulation — is depleted. This has been described as the **"compliance cost"**: the accumulated toll of redirecting finite cognitive resources away from authentic self-regulation toward performing neurotypicality.

### Masking, burnout, and the compounding toll

Stim suppression is a core component of autistic masking. **Hull et al. (2017)** identified three components of camouflaging: compensation (strategies to overcome difficulties), masking (strategies to hide autism), and assimilation (strategies to blend in). Hull et al. (2021, *Molecular Autism*) found that camouflaging was linearly associated with **greater symptoms of generalized anxiety, depression, and social anxiety**, with likelihood of poor mental health increasing across the entire range of camouflaging scores.

**Raymaker et al. (2020)** published the first empirical study on autistic burnout in *Autism in Adulthood*, defining it as "a syndrome resulting from chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports, characterised by pervasive, long-term exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus." Masking autistic traits — including stimming — was identified as a **primary contributor**. The study, conducted as community-based participatory research through AASPIRE, explicitly warned: "Our data underscores the potential dangers of teaching autistic people to mask or suppress their autistic traits." Some participants related **increased suicidal ideation** during burnout.

Research documents that camouflaging often begins in childhood and persists across the lifespan, with cumulative effects. Wiskerke et al. (2018) found that **80% of autistic adults** reported trying to hide stims "often or always" in adulthood, with 60% also doing so in childhood. Late-diagnosed autistic women associate camouflaging with exhaustion and identity confusion (Bargiela et al., 2016).

### ABA's history and the "quiet hands" legacy

The historical treatment of stimming in Applied Behavior Analysis represents the most systematic institutionalization of stim suppression. **O. Ivar Lovaas** established the Young Autism Project clinic at UCLA in 1962, prescribing 40 hours per week of one-to-one instruction for 2–3 years. Early methods included physical aversives — electric shocks, slaps, verbal reprimands — for self-stimulatory behavior. In 1965, *Life* magazine featured Lovaas's work under the title **"Screams, Slaps, and Love."** The explicit goal was making autistic children "indistinguishable from their typically developing peers" (McEachin, Smith, and Lovaas, 1993). Lovaas wrote about autistic children as lacking humanity, stating they needed to be "rebuilt as proper humans" (Lovaas, Schaeffer, and Simmons, 1965).

**"Quiet hands"** became the phrase that encapsulated this approach: a compliance prompt directing autistic children to stop all stimming, especially hand-flapping, enforced through physical restraint if the child did not comply. Julia Bascom's 2011 essay "Quiet Hands," published on her blog *Just Stimming…*, became the foundational text of the anti-suppression movement. Her words — "When I was a little girl, I was autistic. And when you're autistic, it's not abuse. It's therapy" — galvanized the community and led to the *Loud Hands* anthology (2012), using "loud hands" as a direct counter-metaphor.

Specific behavioral techniques used to suppress stimming include extinction, response interruption and redirection (RIRD), response blocking, differential reinforcement of "appropriate" alternative behaviors, and punishment. **Kupferstein (2018)** found that **46% of ABA-exposed respondents met the diagnostic threshold for PTSD**, with children having a 130% increased chance of meeting PTSD criteria. (This study has received an Expression of Concern for methodological limitations, but remains widely cited within the autistic community as reflecting lived experience.) Sandoval-Norton and Shkedy (2019) questioned ABA for possible human rights violations. Wilkenfeld and McCarthy (2020) raised bioethics concerns. The comparison between ABA and conversion therapy is explicitly drawn by multiple autistic scholars — notably, Lovaas himself participated in the "Feminine Boy Project," which used similar behavioral techniques to attempt conversion therapy on gender-nonconforming children (Rekers and Lovaas, 1974).

Some contemporary ABA practitioners have moved toward abandoning aversives, using naturalistic teaching methods, and acknowledging that non-harmful stimming should not be targeted. However, critics argue these shifts are insufficient — the underlying behavioral philosophy of compliance and normalization persists.

### Mental health outcomes and minority stress

**Cassidy, Bradley, Shaw, and Baron-Cohen (2018)** identified camouflaging as a **unique risk marker for suicidality** in autistic adults, explaining significant additional variance beyond known risk factors. Cassidy et al. (2020) connected camouflaging to thwarted belongingness and suicidal thoughts through the Interpersonal Psychological Theory of Suicide. Bradley et al. (2021, *Autism in Adulthood*) found qualitatively that camouflaging made participants more likely to have mental health breakdowns, suicidal thoughts, or engage in self-injury.

**Botha and Frost (2020)** — autistic-led research — applied the **minority stress model** to autism, finding that everyday discrimination, internalized stigma, and concealment (including stim suppression) significantly predicted poorer mental health, even after controlling for general stress exposure. This reframes poor mental health from being "inherent to autism" to being a product of social marginalization. As Botha, Dibb, and Frost (2022, *Disability and Society*) found, autistic participants rejected the idea that suffering was inherent to autism, instead describing it as "a product of not being neurotypical in a world that demands it."

## How autistic people reclaimed stimming as culture

### From clinical target to community celebration

The reclamation of stimming represents one of the neurodiversity movement's most powerful cultural shifts. Where clinicians counted stims as diagnostic evidence of disorder, autistic communities have reframed them as natural, purposeful, and often joyful. This transformation happened through writing, activism, art, and digital community-building.

**Mel Baggs** (1980–2020), a non-binary, non-speaking autistic writer and disability rights advocate, posted the 8-minute YouTube video **"In My Language"** in January 2007. The first half showed Baggs interacting with their environment — rocking, humming, rubbing hands on surfaces, manipulating objects — their "native language." The second half, narrated through typing, challenged viewers to recognize these interactions as "a constant conversation with every aspect of my environment," not meaningless behavior. The video went viral, was covered by CNN and *Wired*, and is recognized as a landmark in demonstrating that stimming constitutes rich, meaningful engagement with the world. Steve Silberman featured Baggs prominently in *NeuroTribes* (2015).

The **stim toy ecosystem** crossed into mainstream culture around 2017 with the fidget spinner craze. Objects originally designed for autistic and ADHD self-regulation — fidget cubes (Kickstarter, 2016), Pop-Its, Tangle toys, chewable jewelry, stress balls — became ubiquitous. **Stimtastic**, founded by autistic writer Cynthia Kim, and **The Autistic Innovator** (founded 2021, 100% autistic- and ADHD-owned) represent community-run businesses centering autistic needs. The mainstream crossover had mixed effects: one autistic child felt less self-conscious about stimming when classmates carried fidget tools, but schools that banned fidget spinners as disruptive risked "isolating the very children they'd spent years trying to include."

Online, a vibrant stimming culture thrives. **Tumblr** developed dedicated "stim blogs" curating visual stim content — slime videos, soap cutting, satisfying textures — with "stim boards" (themed collages of sensory-rich GIFs) becoming a recognized art form. **"Stimmy"** emerged as a positive descriptor ("this is so stimmy!"). On **YouTube**, channels like STIMMY create long stim videos "for people who can't or don't want to buy stim toys of their own, or who feel unsafe stimming in public." Amythest Schaber's "Ask an Autistic" series provided foundational stimming education. On **TikTok**, hashtags including #stimming, #stimmingisbeautiful, and #stimmingpride have accumulated millions of views, with autistic creators celebrating vocal stimming, stim dancing, and authentic movement.

### Stimming at the intersection of race and safety

The freedom to stim in public is not equally distributed. For Black, Indigenous, and other people of color who are autistic, stimming carries additional, sometimes life-threatening risk. Autistic behaviors — repetitive movements, avoiding eye contact, not responding to verbal commands, unusual vocalizations — can be misinterpreted by police as intoxication, noncompliance, or threat. For racially marginalized people, these misinterpretations layer onto existing racial profiling. The risk is not additive but **multiplicative**.

Statistics underscore this reality: **50% of people killed by US law enforcement are disabled** (Center for American Progress). More than half of disabled African Americans have been arrested by age 28 — double the rate of white disabled counterparts. Disabled people are at least 7 times more likely to encounter police than nondisabled people.

Documented cases illustrate the danger. In 2019, **Elijah McClain**, a young Black man in Aurora, Colorado, was killed after a police encounter; community advocates noted his behavior — wearing a ski mask for sensory reasons, being described as "different" — may reflect neurodivergent traits. In 2024, **Ryan Gainer**, a 15-year-old autistic Black teen in Apple Valley, California, was shot and killed by a sheriff's deputy at his home after a family crisis call. Steve Silberman, writing in the *New York Times* about the case of Connor Leibel — a white autistic teen handcuffed after police mistook his stimming for intoxication — asked readers to "imagine if instead of being fair-haired and rail-thin, Connor had been powerfully built and black or Hispanic."

**Morénike Giwa Onaiwu**, a Nigerian-American autistic woman, has been a leading voice at this intersection. She co-edited (with Lydia X. Z. Brown and E. Ashkenazy) *All the Weight of Our Dreams: On Living Racialized Autism* (2017, DragonBee Press) — the first-ever anthology on race and autism. She wrote: "It's never 'just' autism for us, and it's never 'just' race. Intersectionality, for us, isn't an intriguing concept to have philosophical debates about; it's our real lives." Her 2020 essay "I, Too, Sing Neurodiversity" in *Ought: The Journal of Autistic Culture* analyzed microaggressions toward neurodivergent people of color and called for anti-racism within the neurodiversity movement. **Lydia X. Z. Brown**, a nonbinary Chinese American autistic activist and attorney, co-led *All the Weight of Our Dreams*, founded the Fund for Community Reparations for Autistic People of Color, and as a high school student drafted Massachusetts legislation requiring autism training for law enforcement. Flanagan et al. (2025, *Autism*) studied 43 Black caregivers of autistic children, finding overwhelming concerns about autistic behaviors being inappropriately policed and explicit threats of harm or murder during encounters.

## Seminal works and the scholars who built this field

This section documents the key texts and researchers — prioritizing autistic-led and neurodivergent-led scholarship — that have shaped understanding of stimming.

### Foundational autistic-led research

**Kapp, S. K., Steward, R., Crane, L., Elliott, D., Elphick, C., Pellicano, E., and Russell, G. (2019).** "'People should be allowed to do what they like': Autistic adults' views and experiences of stimming." *Autism*, 23(7), 1782–1792. The first in-depth qualitative study of stimming from autistic perspectives. Co-produced with autistic adults through interviews and focus groups with 32 participants. Identified two themes: stimming as a self-regulatory mechanism, and stimming lacking social acceptance but becoming accepted through understanding. Key conclusion: "The point of intervention could be shifted to the overwhelming environment rather than the autistic person themselves." Steven Kapp is an autistic researcher at the University of Exeter.

**Raymaker, D. M., et al. (2020).** "'Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew': Defining Autistic Burnout." *Autism in Adulthood*, 2(2), 132–143. The first empirical study of autistic burnout, conducted as community-based participatory research. Identified masking — including stim suppression — as a primary contributor to burnout. Dora Raymaker is an autistic researcher at Portland State University.

**Morris, I. F., et al. (2025).** "Beyond self-regulation: Autistic experiences and perceptions of stimming." *Autism and Developmental Language Impairments*. Extended Kapp's work to establish stimming's social-communicative function and its role in community connection among autistic people.

**Botha, M., and Frost, D. M. (2020).** "Extending the minority stress model to understand mental health problems experienced by the autistic population." *Society and Mental Health*, 10(1), 20–34. Autistic-led research applying minority stress theory to autism, identifying concealment (including stim suppression) as a specific stressor. Monique Botha is an autistic researcher.

### Key neurodivergent scholars and their contributions

**Damian Milton** — autistic autism researcher, University of Kent. His 2012 paper "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'" (*Disability and Society*, 27(6), 883–887) challenged the assumption that communication difficulties are a one-sided autistic deficit, proposing instead a mutual disjuncture between differently disposed social actors. Directly relevant to stimming: non-autistic people's failure to read stimming as meaningful reflects their empathy gap. A 10-year retrospective (Milton, Gurbuz, and López, 2022) confirmed substantial empirical support, including Crompton's research showing autistic-autistic dyads communicate effectively.

**Nick Walker** — queer, transgender, autistic writer; professor of psychology at California Institute of Integral Studies; co-founder of Autonomous Press. *Neuroqueer Heresies: Notes on the Neurodiversity Paradigm, Autistic Empowerment, and Postnormal Possibilities* (2021, Autonomous Press) includes a chapter titled "On the Practice of Stimming," discussing the "policing of hands" and how consciously freeing oneself to stim is an act of "neuroqueering." Walker coined the term "neuroqueer" in 2008 and is responsible for foundational definitions within the neurodiversity paradigm. Walker's essay "Throw Away the Master's Tools" argues for complete rejection of pathologizing frameworks.

**Devon Price** — autistic and transgender social psychologist, Loyola University Chicago. *Unmasking Autism: Discovering the New Faces of Neurodiversity* (2022, Harmony/Penguin Random House) documents the costs of masking, including stim suppression, and provides practical guidance for unmasking. On NPR: "If I'm safe flapping my hands down the street, so are people with schizophrenia or mobile disabilities. It's a world that is safe for everyone."

**Julia Bascom** — autistic writer, executive director of ASAN. "Quiet Hands" (2011, *Just Stimming…* blog) is the foundational text on the trauma of stim suppression in ABA. Editor of *Loud Hands: Autistic People, Speaking* (2012, Autistic Press). Her blog name — "Just Stimming" — itself represents reclamation: "because all I'm doing is lining up words, just stimming... It's hard work. It's important to me. It's significant."

**Mel Baggs** (1980–2020) — non-binary, non-speaking autistic writer and activist. "In My Language" (2007, YouTube) demonstrated that stimming constitutes rich engagement with the world. Coined "Aspie supremacy" on their blog *Ballastexistenz*. Lydia X. Z. Brown stated at their passing: "Sie fought unceasingly for the principles that all people count, regardless of type or degree of disability."

**M. Remi Yergeau** — autistic scholar. *Authoring Autism: On Rhetoric and Neurological Queerness* (2018, Duke University Press) explores how autistic people are silenced through rhetorical frameworks. Challenged declarations of stimming as necessarily involuntary.

### The DSM framing and its critique

The DSM-5 (2013) and DSM-5-TR (2022) classify stimming under criterion B for Autism Spectrum Disorder: "Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities," specifically B1 ("Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech") and the newly added B4 ("Hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment"). The inclusion of sensory features in B4 was an important advance — sensory differences were absent from DSM-IV criteria entirely.

Community critique centers on the **deficit-based framing**. The DSM describes autistic traits using terms like "deficits," "abnormal," "restricted," and "stereotyped" — language positioning autism as inherently disordered. Critics note that "restricted interests" pathologizes deep, passionate focus; "stereotyped" implies meaninglessness; and severity levels measure "how inconveniencing it is for the people around us, rather than how much support we need." Bottema-Beutel, Kapp, et al. (2021) systematically documented ableist language in autism research and provided alternatives in their landmark paper in *Autism in Adulthood*.

## What to call it, what to avoid, and why language matters

Language in this domain carries history. Every term choice reflects a theoretical framework — pathology or neurodiversity, elimination or acceptance, compliance or autonomy.

**Preferred terms:**

- **"Stimming" / "stims"** — the widely preferred term in autistic communities; neutral to positive connotations; reclaimed and redefined by the community it describes
- **"Repetitive behaviors/movements"** — neutral descriptive language acceptable in research contexts
- **"Self-regulation strategies"** — when discussing function; centers purpose rather than appearance

**Terms to avoid:**

- **"Stereotypy" / "stereotypic behavior"** — rooted in Skinner's behavioral tradition, inherently framed as "nonfunctional" behavior requiring modification, associated with elimination-focused practices, and carrying the dehumanizing legacy of early research on institutionalized people
- **"Self-stimulatory behavior"** — while technically the origin of "stimming," the full clinical form implies stimming is solely about providing sensory stimulation, oversimplifying its diverse functions; use "stimming" instead
- **"Inappropriate" / "aberrant behavior"** — value-laden; inappropriate by whose standard?
- **"Purposeless" / "nonfunctional"** — directly contradicted by autistic testimony and empirical research
- **"Eliminate" / "extinguish"** (in reference to stimming) — frames natural behaviors as problems to be removed

The ND Connection language guide notes: "Stereotypy comes from a behavioural root based on the works of B.F. Skinner. When used in the context of Neurodivergence, stereotypy is a behaviour that is often viewed as a negative to be regulated and corrected." Webster's definition includes "typically purposeless" — a characterization that decades of autistic scholarship has demonstrated to be wrong.

## Conclusion: stimming as a right, not a concession

Three insights emerge from this research that should anchor any neurodivergent-affirming understanding of stimming.

First, **stimming is neurologically grounded and multiply functional.** It is not a quirk, a deficit, or a distraction. It is a homeostatic mechanism involving dopamine regulation, GABA-mediated sensory modulation, endorphin release, and autonomic nervous system management. It supports arousal regulation, emotional expression, cognitive processing, social communication among autistic people, and the simple human experience of sensory pleasure. Abrahamson et al.'s (2024) reframing of "stimming as thinking" represents a paradigm shift: stimming is cognition, not its absence.

Second, **the harm of suppression is not hypothetical — it is empirically documented.** Stim suppression depletes cognitive resources, reduces self-efficacy, contributes to autistic burnout, and is identified as a unique risk factor for suicidality. The 84% of autistic adults who report being told not to stim represent a population-level exposure to a practice that research consistently links to poor outcomes. The intervention target should be the overwhelming environment, not the autistic person.

Third, **the community's reclamation of stimming is itself a form of knowledge production.** From Mel Baggs' "In My Language" to Julia Bascom's "Quiet Hands" to the collective scholarship correcting neurodiversity's origins, autistic people have not merely provided "lived experience" to supplement clinical research — they have generated the theoretical frameworks, the empirical questions, and the language through which stimming is now understood. The field's trajectory from Lovaas's "quiet hands" to Kapp's "people should be allowed to do what they like" was driven by autistic scholars, writers, and activists who insisted that their own bodies were not the problem. Respecting stimming is not accommodation. It is recognition.
