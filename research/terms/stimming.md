# Stimming: A Comprehensive Guide to Self-Stimulatory Behavior

---

**Abstract** — Stimming — the repetitive performance of movements, sounds, or other sensory-rich behaviors — is a fundamental aspect of autistic neurology, not a symptom to be eliminated. Rooted in sensory modulation, emotional regulation, and cognitive processing, stimming serves as a homeostatic mechanism that helps autistic people navigate a world built for different nervous systems. The term itself was reclaimed by autistic communities in the 1990s from the clinical language of Applied Behavior Analysis, transforming "self-stimulatory behavior" from a target for elimination into a celebrated expression of neurodivergent identity. Decades of research — increasingly led by autistic scholars — now confirm what the community has long known: suppressing stimming causes measurable psychological harm, while accepting it supports wellbeing, self-regulation, and authentic connection. This document synthesizes neuroscience, autistic-led scholarship, community history, and clinical critique. It prioritizes autistic voices, empirical evidence, and the principle that understanding stimming begins with listening to those who do it.

---

## I. Where "Stimming" Came From and How It Was Reclaimed

The word **"stimming"** is a clipped form of "self-stimming," itself shortened from "self-stimulatory behavior" (SSB). The Oxford English Dictionary records the **earliest known use of the noun "stimming" in 1983**, in the *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis*, where it described "nonvocal, inappropriate repetitive movement consisting of picking, tapping or spinning objects, hand flapping, stroking lips, hand clenching accompanied by facial contortions, or running back and forth" [1]. Dictionary.com dates first recorded use to 1980–85.

The clinical term "self-stimulatory behavior" emerged from the **Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) tradition at UCLA** in the late 1960s and early 1970s, primarily through the work of O. I. Lovaas and colleagues. Key early publications include Lovaas, Litrownik, and Mann (1971) on response latencies in autistic children engaged in self-stimulatory behavior [2], and Koegel and Covert (1972) in *JABA*, which used punishment — including "slapping the subject briskly on the hands" — to suppress what they termed "self-stimulation" [3]. T. Attwood has noted that the term was coined to describe behaviors observed in autistic people "living on a ward in a government institution with spartan furniture, almost no recreational facilities and staff who were not trained." The clinical framework attributed these behaviors to individual pathology rather than environmental deprivation.

**Autistic self-advocates reclaimed "stimming" in online community spaces during the 1990s and early 2000s**, through forums like Autism Network International (ANI), founded by Jim Sinclair, and later through the Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN). As Kapp et al. (2019) documented, "Autistic adults have led resistance to these efforts [to eliminate stims], reclaiming 'self-stimulatory behaviour' as 'stimming'" [4]. This reclamation reframed stimming from a dysfunctional symptom into a purposeful, adaptive behavior serving legitimate neurological needs — self-regulation, emotional expression, sensory processing, and communication. Scholar-activists including M. R. Yergeau (2018) challenged declarations of stimming as necessarily involuntary [5], while Nolan and McBride (2015) denounced attempts to reduce autistic bodily autonomy [6].

The related clinical term **"stereotypy"** (from Greek *stereos* + *typos*, "fixed impression") has been used in psychiatry since the 19th century to describe repetitive, seemingly purposeless movements. It is considered problematic by many autistic people because it originates in B. F. Skinner's behavioral tradition, implies purposelessness (directly contradicting autistic testimony), and is deeply intertwined with ABA practices aimed at elimination. The term "restricted and repetitive behaviors" (RRBs) became the standard clinical umbrella from DSM-IV onward, though the neurodiversity community critiques the word "restricted" as deficit-framing of deep, focused interests [71].

## II. What Happens in the Brain During Stimming

The neuroscience of stimming involves multiple interconnected brain systems, neurotransmitter pathways, and sensory processing mechanisms. While research is ongoing, a coherent picture has emerged of stimming as a neurologically grounded regulatory behavior.

### A. Neural Circuitry

The **basal ganglia** are central to stimming. Cortico-striatal projections to the dorsal striatum convey input from motor, cognitive, and limbic cortices. Neuroimaging studies have identified increased striatal volume — particularly the **caudate nucleus** — correlating with repetitive behaviors in autism [7], [8]. S. Grossberg and D. Kishnan (2018) proposed that when basal ganglia gates in the substantia nigra remain open too long, downstream recurrent circuits persistently oscillate, triggering repetitive motor behaviors [9]. Postmortem studies found **increased D2 receptor expression** in medium spiny neurons of the caudate and putamen in autistic individuals, implicating the indirect basal ganglia pathway [10].

The **cerebellum** plays a significant role. H. S. Singer et al. (2021) found statistically significant reduction in white matter volume of posterior cerebellar lobules VI–VII that negatively correlated with motor control, alongside an 8% increase in anterior vermis gray matter positively correlated with stereotypy severity scores [11]. The **prefrontal cortex** is implicated through executive processing and dopamine-based modulation, with fronto-striatal functional connectivity showing significant positive association with repetitive behavior severity. Research from the University of Rochester using EEG found that autistic participants showed reduced predictive attenuation during self-initiated touch compared to neurotypical controls — potentially driving the need for repetitive, predictable self-generated sensory feedback [12].

### B. Neurotransmitter Systems

**Dopamine** is the most extensively studied neurotransmitter in relation to stimming. The dopamine hypothesis of autism posits that nigrostriatal circuit dysfunction is associated with stereotyped behaviors, while mesocorticolimbic dysfunction relates to social differences [13]. A Karolinska Institutet study demonstrated that mice with the autism-risk gene eIF4E showed reduced dopamine release in the basal ganglia due to impaired acetylcholine-dopamine interactions [14]. Stimming likely stimulates dopamine release, which can decrease glutamate levels, calming the brain and providing comfort. Because stimming produces both sensory feedback and neurochemical reward, it becomes self-reinforcing.

**GABA**, the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter, shows **reduced levels** in autism — a central feature of the excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) imbalance theory. Lower sensorimotor GABA levels are associated with tactile hypersensitivity [15]. Reduced GABA in the left ventral premotor cortex correlates with sensory hyper-responsiveness [16]. This E/I imbalance helps explain why autistic nervous systems are more reactive to sensory input and correspondingly more dependent on regulatory strategies like stimming.

The **endogenous opioid system** offers another mechanism: the endorphin hypothesis proposes that certain stimming actions trigger beta-endorphin release, producing pleasurable feelings and pain reduction. Studies of opioid antagonists (naloxone, naltrexone) in autistic individuals showed reduction in self-aggressive behavior and hyperactivity, supporting this pathway [17]. **Serotonin** also plays a role, with hyperserotonemia found in approximately 25–30% of autistic individuals [18].

### C. Stimming as Arousal Regulation

Stimming functions as arousal regulation consistent with the **Yerkes-Dodson inverted-U model**: when overstimulated, stimming blocks or modulates sensory input to bring arousal down; when understimulated, it provides additional input to raise arousal toward the optimal zone. E. M. Ornitz and E. R. Ritvo (1968) described autism as a syndrome of "perceptual inconstancy," with motor output needed to modulate inconsistent sensory input [19]. More recent predictive processing frameworks suggest stimming provides familiar, reliable self-generated feedback in response to difficulties with unpredictable stimuli [20], [21].

**Sensory hyperresponsiveness predicts approximately 51% of the variance in repetitive behaviors**, with sensory-seeking behaviors accounting for an additional 11% [22]. Kapp et al. (2019) found that **72% of autistic adults** report stimming helps with anxiety, **57%** with overstimulation, and **69%** to calm down [4].

### D. Sensory Processing Across the Spectrum

W. Dunn's (1997) Four Quadrant Model provides a foundational framework for understanding stimming across sensory profiles, organized along two axes — neurological threshold (high to low) and behavioral response (passive to active) [23]:

- **Sensation seeking** (high threshold, active response): Actively pursuing more sensory input — the "I need more" state that drives stimming for arousal
- **Sensation avoiding** (low threshold, active response): Actively limiting sensory input — the "too much" state where stimming blocks overwhelming stimuli
- **Sensory sensitivity** (low threshold, passive response): Detecting more input and becoming easily distracted
- **Low registration** (high threshold, passive response): Not noticing stimuli, requiring stronger input

Studies using the Sensory Profile consistently find significantly higher sensory processing differences in autistic individuals, with hearing and touch showing the most marked patterns [24]. The same individual may stim differently depending on their current sensory state — seeking proprioceptive input through jumping when under-aroused, or rocking rhythmically to create predictable input when overwhelmed.

### E. The Autonomic Nervous System and Polyvagal Connections

S. W. Porges' **Polyvagal Theory** proposes three hierarchical neural circuits: the ventral vagal complex (social engagement, safety), sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight), and dorsal vagal complex (shutdown, immobilization). Applied to stimming, the theory suggests that autistic people may use self-stimulatory behaviors to calm their sympathetic nervous system and maintain regulation [25]. Vestibular stimulation and proprioceptive input may serve as "neural exercises of state regulation."

However, important caveats exist. A 2022 systematic review argued that autonomic nervous system dysfunction may not be a dominant feature of autism, finding most autistic children have normal parasympathetic baseline values [26]. From the neurodiversity perspective, autistic scholar-activist "Trauma Geek" offers a critical reframe: while the core concepts of polyvagal theory remain useful, the idea that autism equals a deficit of social engagement function pathologizes autistic people. They argue autistic people may have "highly attuned or sensitive neuroception" rather than "faulty neuroception," and that low vagal function often reflects trauma history rather than inherent trait.

### F. How Autistic Stimming Differs from Neurotypical Fidgeting

**All humans stim.** Foot tapping, hair twirling, pen clicking, and nail biting are universal self-regulatory behaviors. The difference between autistic stimming and neurotypical fidgeting is best understood as one of **degree, intensity, breadth, and functional necessity** rather than of kind:

- **Intensity and frequency**: Neurotypical fidgeting is usually short-lived and less noticeable; autistic stimming is often more sustained and visible
- **Breadth of sensory involvement**: Fidgeting primarily involves tactile stimulation, while autistic stimming encompasses auditory, visual, vestibular, proprioceptive, and olfactory senses
- **Physiological necessity**: Autistic people describe stimming as a physiological need; suppression requires extraordinary effort and causes distress [4]
- **Underlying neurology**: Autistic brains have fundamentally different sensory processing thresholds — lower GABA [15], [16], altered dopamine signaling [13], different connectivity patterns — creating both greater need for self-regulation and more intense expression of regulatory behaviors

## III. The Sensory Landscape of Stimming Types

Stimming spans every sensory modality. Understanding the full range — rather than fixating on the most visible forms — is essential for recognizing and supporting autistic self-regulation.

**Visual stimming** engages the sense of sight: moving fingers in front of eyes to filter light, watching spinning objects (fans, wheels, coins), staring at patterns or flowing water, excessive blinking, turning lights on and off, gazing at lava lamps, and lining up objects for visual satisfaction.

**Auditory stimming** engages hearing: humming tunes, listening to the same song on repeat, clicking or snapping fingers, tapping ears or objects, covering and uncovering ears, and making repetitive sounds. Auditory processing in autism shows atypical patterns, with the nervous system processing environmental sounds differently [27].

**Tactile stimming** engages touch: rubbing textures, stroking particular fabrics, skin picking, playing with fidget toys, running fingers over surfaces, squeezing objects, and hair twirling. Hearing and touch show the most pronounced sensory processing differences in autism [28], [29], [30].

**Vestibular stimming** engages balance and spatial orientation: body rocking (back and forth, side to side), spinning the whole body, swinging, bouncing on toes or trampolines, pacing, and head rolling. The rhythm of rocking behaviors is often around **60 beats per minute** — possibly synchronizing with and reducing heart rate [31].

**Proprioceptive stimming** engages body awareness through muscles and joints: jumping, seeking deep pressure (bear hugs, weighted blankets), clenching and relaxing muscles, pressing against walls, heavy lifting, wearing tight clothing or weighted vests, toe walking, and crashing into soft surfaces.

**Olfactory and gustatory stimming** engages smell and taste: sniffing objects, licking surfaces, chewing (chewable jewelry, pencils, gum), biting nails or lips, tasting textures, and grinding teeth. Oral stimming is particularly common and is served by a growing market of chewable stim tools.

**Vocal and echolalic stimming** engages voice and speech patterns: echolalia (repeating heard speech, present in **75–80% of verbal autistic individuals** [32]), scripting (repeating lines from media), palilalia (repeating one's own words), humming melodies, and making repetitive vocalizations. Modern research has overturned the view that echolalia is "meaningless repetition." Pascual et al. (2023) found that **all echolalia usages by autistic participants were functional and communicative** [33]. B. M. Prizant and J. F. Duchan (1981) identified functions including turn-taking, affirmation, requests, and self-regulation [34].

**Interoceptive dimensions** involve awareness of internal body states — heartbeat, hunger, pain, temperature. Impaired interoceptive processing in autism may contribute to difficulty identifying emotional states (alexithymia), and stimming may serve as a way to generate detectable sensory feedback when internal signals are unclear.

## IV. What Stimming Actually Does: Regulation, Expression, Cognition, and Joy

Stimming is not one thing. It is a multi-functional behavior that serves at least six distinct purposes, often simultaneously.

### A. Self-Regulation and Arousal Modulation

The regulatory function is the best-documented. Kapp et al. (2019) found that autistic adults report stimming helps manage anxiety (72%), overstimulation (57%), and emotional intensity generally (69%) [4]. Temple Grandin described her rocking as "a way to shut out the world when it was too intense" [35]. R. A. Charlton et al. (2021) found that autistic participants described stimming as involuntary bodily responses that communicate emotional states even when they lack words for them [42]. Morris et al. (2025) reported that autistic participants consistently described stimming as indispensable for self-regulation, with suppression causing emotional dysregulation and distress [38].

### B. Cognitive Enhancement and Flow State Facilitation

Emerging research suggests stimming actively supports cognition — particularly focus, memory retrieval, and executive function — rather than merely distracting from thinking. Abrahamson et al. (2024) proposed "stimming as thinking" — that self-stimulatory behavior functions as an **epistemic resource** that supports embodied cognition, allowing autistic people to maintain attention and process information more effectively [40]. This aligns with monotropism theory (Murray, Lesser, and Lawson, 2005), which argues that autistic cognitive processing involves deep, intense tunnels of attention; stimming may help maintain and deepen these attention tunnels.

Heasman et al. (2024) extended this to flow theory, exploring the relationship between monotropism, flow states, and stimming — proposing that stimming can serve as both a facilitator of and expression of autistic flow [41]. The implication is significant: stimming is not distraction from thinking but potentially integral to it.

### C. Emotional Expression and Communication

Stimming communicates internal states — particularly when verbal expression is unavailable or insufficient. Autistic people frequently describe specific stims as externally visible emotional signals: hand flapping as excitement or joy, rocking as distress or self-soothing, particular vocalizations as contentment. Julia Bascom's widely circulated essay "Quiet Hands" (2011) described stimming as "autistic body language" — a form of communication that neurotypical people are not equipped to read [51]. Mel Baggs' viral video "In My Language" (2007) powerfully demonstrated how stimming constitutes a complete and meaningful language of engagement with the world [62].

### D. Sensory Pleasure and Joy

Not all stimming is regulatory in a distress-reduction sense. Much stimming is pleasurably motivated — pursued because it feels good. Kapp et al. (2019) found that **96%** of autistic participants report stimming for pleasure or enjoyment, making it the most commonly endorsed function [4]. This aligns with the neurochemical picture: stimming produces dopamine and potentially endorphins, creating genuine positive feeling states. The neurodiversity community has embraced "stimming for joy" as important to affirm, pushing back against clinical framings that treat all stimming as a regulatory response to distress.

### E. Pain Reduction and Somatic Relief

Some stimming serves analgesic functions. Repetitive movement may reduce perception of pain through multiple mechanisms: generating competing sensory input, triggering endorphin release, or providing proprioceptive feedback that grounds body awareness. Autistic people with chronic pain conditions frequently report stims that specifically address somatic discomfort.

### F. Identity and Community Expression

For many autistic people, stimming has become an identity marker — a visible expression of autistic neurotype that, when shared, creates community recognition and belonging. The concept of "stim parties" (gatherings where stimming is encouraged and celebrated) reflects stimming as cultural practice, not just regulatory necessity. Charlton et al. (2021) found that many autistic adults describe their stims as core aspects of selfhood — "just part of who I am" [42].

## V. Stim Suppression: Costs and Harms

### A. The Demand to Suppress

Autistic people face pervasive social pressure to suppress stimming — from ABA therapy formally targeting stims for elimination, to informal social policing ("stop fidgeting," "sit still"), to internalized shame about "looking weird." The history of stim suppression is inseparable from the history of ABA: Lovaas and Koegel's early work used aversives including electric shock to eliminate self-stimulatory behaviors [3], and the goal persisted even as methods became less overtly punitive.

### B. Psychological and Neurological Costs

The costs of suppression are well-documented. Kapp et al. (2019) found that autistic adults who suppress stims report increased anxiety, emotional dysregulation, and exhaustion [4]. The effort of suppression depletes cognitive and emotional resources needed for other tasks — consistent with ego depletion models [43]. Charlton et al. (2021) found that stim suppression was associated with reduced self-efficacy and greater mental health burden [44]. Raymaker et al. (2020) identified stim suppression as a core component of the chronic resource depletion that produces autistic burnout [47].

### C. Camouflaging and Its Consequences

Stim suppression is a central component of **autistic camouflaging** — the broader phenomenon of masking autistic traits to appear neurotypical. Hull et al. (2017, 2021) found that camouflaging is associated with significantly higher rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation [45], [46]. Cassidy et al. (2018) identified camouflaging as a significant independent risk factor for suicidality in autistic adults [57]. Wiskerke et al. (2018) found camouflaging is more prevalent in autistic women, which may partially explain delayed diagnosis [48]. Bargiela, Steward, and Mandy (2016) found late-diagnosed autistic women had spent decades suppressing their autistic traits, including stimming, at enormous psychological cost [49].

Botha and Frost (2020) extended the minority stress model to autism, arguing that the social pressure to camouflage — including stim suppression — functions as a chronic minority stressor that explains elevated mental health difficulties independent of autistic traits themselves [60]. Botha, Dibb, and Frost (2022) found that internalized stigma, including shame about stimming, was associated with poorer autistic identity and wellbeing outcomes [61].

### D. ABA's Specific Harms

ABA therapy has targeted stim suppression as a central goal since its inception. Kupferstein (2018) found elevated PTSD symptoms in autistic people exposed to ABA, with effect sizes comparable to those found in combat veterans [53]. Sandoval-Norton and Shkedy (2019) argued that intensive ABA constitutes a form of abuse through its systematic suppression of autistic-authentic behavior [54]. Wilkenfeld and McCarthy (2020) outlined the ethical problems with ABA's foundational compliance orientation, including the targeting of self-regulatory behaviors for elimination [55]. The Rekers and Lovaas (1974) case — using ABA-derived methods to suppress gender-nonconforming behavior in a child who later died by suicide — illustrates the broader pattern of ABA targeting authentic self-expression [56].

## VI. The Social and Environmental Context of Stimming

### A. Double Empathy and Neurotype Translation

D. E. M. Milton's **Double Empathy Problem** (2012) provides a foundational reframe for understanding the social dimension of stimming [39]. Rather than locating the "problem" of stimming in the autistic individual, the double empathy framework recognizes that difficulties arise from bidirectional empathy gaps between neurotypes. Neurotypical people often find stimming uncomfortable, distracting, or stigmatizing — but this reflects neurotypical processing norms, not any inherent wrongness in stimming itself. Milton, Gurbuz, and López (2022) noted that ten years after the original paper, the double empathy framework has substantially shifted scholarly understanding of autistic social difference [65].

### B. Race, Disability, and Stimming

The social perception of stimming is not race-neutral. Stimming behaviors that overlap with stereotyped racial fears — particularly repetitive movements, sudden sounds, or unpredictable physical behaviors — place Black and Brown autistic people at elevated risk of police violence. Flanagan et al. (2025) documented the experiences of Black caregivers of autistic children navigating police interactions, noting that behaviors functioning as stims were frequently misread as threatening [68]. The intersection of autism, race, and stimming represents an underdeveloped area of research with urgent practical implications.

### C. Gender and Late Diagnosis

Autistic women and gender-diverse people disproportionately internalize stim suppression as a survival strategy, contributing to diagnostic invisibility. The social expectation that girls and women regulate visible emotional and behavioral expression more completely than boys and men aligns with camouflaging demands; autistic girls learn to suppress stimming earlier and more thoroughly, rendering their autism less "visible" and delaying diagnosis. Bargiela et al. (2016) documented this pattern extensively in their qualitative study of late-diagnosed women [49].

### D. Workplace and Institutional Contexts

Autistic adults face workplace norms that pathologize stimming — open offices, expectation of stillness in meetings, professional dress codes that preclude comfortable sensory clothing, and social policing of visible self-regulation. The result is chronic suppression in professional settings, contributing to autistic burnout and workplace attrition. Neurodiversity-affirming workplace design — quiet spaces, freedom to move, acceptance of visible stimming — is increasingly recognized as both ethical and beneficial to productivity.

## VII. Therapeutic and Affirmative Approaches

### A. The Shift Toward Affirmative Practice

The therapeutic landscape around stimming has shifted substantially, though unevenly. Occupational therapy, speech-language pathology, and some branches of psychology have moved toward **sensory integration** approaches that work with sensory needs rather than against them [37]. The goal shifts from suppression to accommodation: providing appropriate sensory environments, stim tools, and social acceptance rather than teaching autistic people to hide regulatory behaviors.

### B. Stim Tools and Environmental Design

A robust market of stim tools has developed — fidget spinners, chewable jewelry, weighted blankets, sensory swings, noise-canceling headphones — that serve legitimate regulatory functions. Occupational therapists increasingly incorporate stim tool selection into sensory diet planning. Environmental design principles — reducing fluorescent lighting, providing movement breaks, allowing flexible seating — reduce the regulatory burden that drives distress-based stimming in the first place.

### C. Distinguishing Safe from Potentially Harmful Stimming

Not all stimming requires intervention, but some carries physical risk. **Self-injurious behaviors** (SIBs) — head-banging, skin biting, eye-pressing, self-hitting — may escalate under conditions of extreme distress, sensory deprivation, or communication barriers. The affirming approach distinguishes between stimming that serves regulation (to be supported) and stimming that causes physical harm (to be addressed by meeting the underlying need differently). The goal is never elimination of the regulatory behavior but always finding safer alternatives that meet the same need.

A critical distinction: **stim suppression is never the appropriate response to SIBs**. SIBs indicate unmet needs — typically pain, extreme distress, or communication barriers — and should prompt investigation of those needs rather than behavioral elimination of the symptom.

### D. Communication and Advocacy

Autistic-led advocacy organizations — ASAN, the Autistic Self Advocacy Network, and neurodiversity-affirming groups — have produced substantial resources on stimming rights. The framing is one of **bodily autonomy**: autistic people have the right to regulate their own nervous systems. Accommodations law in the United States (ADA) and international human rights frameworks (UN CRPD General Comment No. 5, 2017) provide legal grounding for reasonable accommodations that allow autistic self-regulation in educational and workplace settings [72].

## VIII. Key Voices and Community Perspectives

Julia Bascom's "Quiet Hands" (2011) and the anthology *Loud Hands* (2012) she edited remain touchstone texts for understanding the autistic community's relationship with stimming — framing the suppression demand as both a practical harm and a form of silencing [51], [52].

Mel Baggs' "In My Language" (2007) demonstrated that stimming constitutes a complete, meaningful engagement with the world, challenging the assumption that neurotypical communication modes are universal or superior [62].

Temple Grandin's accounts of her own stimming and sensory needs, while representing an earlier and more medical-accommodationist perspective, contributed significantly to public awareness that autistic people have genuine sensory experiences that drive repetitive behavior [35].

Lyric Rivera (Neurodivergent Rebel) has produced extensive community-facing content on stimming, distinguishing involuntary "autistic body language" from intentional sensory-seeking and advocating for social acceptance of both.

The collective history of neurodiversity theory — developed through the contributions of Judy Singer, Jim Sinclair, Mel Baggs, and many others — established the conceptual foundation that stimming is not pathology but difference. Botha et al. (2024) corrected the historical record, documenting that neurodiversity theory emerged from collective community development rather than any single originator [69].
